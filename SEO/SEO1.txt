H1

Cockatiel diet: what to feed (safe foods, routine, and what to avoid)

Intro
If you’ve ever Googled “what should I feed my cockatiel?” you’ve probably seen 50 conflicting answers. This guide gives you a simple, practical structure: what to use as the “base,” what to add for variety, and what to keep out of the bowl.

Quick answer (TL;DR)

Build a consistent base (dry food routine) + fresh add-ons (produce mix) for variety.

Keep fresh foods fresh and short-lived (serve, then remove leftovers).

Avoid “human food assumptions”—some common foods can be risky for birds.

Want it personalized? Use Paws & Plates to generate example meals and a meal prep plan.

Safe staples (most common “go-to” choices)

A good cockatiel routine usually has two buckets:

Dry base (the dependable daily baseline)

Fresh foods (variety and enrichment)

Dry base ideas

A consistent, quality dry routine (often pellet-forward in many modern feeding styles)

Seed mixes can exist as part of the routine for some birds, but over-relying on seeds tends to create imbalance—use them thoughtfully.

Fresh foods (variety bucket)

A rotating mix of vegetables (think “chop”-style mixes)

Small amounts of fruit as occasional variety (not the main event)

Foods to limit (because “more” isn’t always better)

High-sugar treats (sweet fruits, sweet snacks)

Very rich or oily foods as frequent staples

Anything heavily salted, seasoned, or processed

Foods to avoid (high-confidence “don’t do it” list)

Keep this list conservative and obvious:

Alcohol

Caffeine (coffee/tea/energy drinks)

Chocolate/cocoa

Xylitol (common sweetener in sugar-free products)

If you’re unsure about a specific food, treat it as “unknown” until you confirm with a trusted avian resource.

A simple daily routine (easy mode)

Morning: Dry base available (or offered in a routine schedule if you prefer timed feeding)

Later: Offer a small fresh portion (chop/veg mix)

Remove leftovers: Don’t leave fresh food sitting out for long periods.

Repeatable > perfect: Consistency beats constant experimentation.

Meal prep + storage (important)

For dry foods (pellets/seed mixes):

Store cool, dry, airtight (not in the fridge).

Moisture is the enemy (stale/mold risk).

For fresh chop:

Prep in small batches.

Keep refrigerated briefly, or freeze portions if you’re prepping ahead.

Serve fresh; remove leftovers promptly.

Switching foods (transition plan)

If you’re changing your cockatiel’s routine:

Transition gradually over 5–7 days.

Make changes small and observe appetite/behavior.

FAQs

Yes—see example meals instantly. Sign up to save pets and generate personalized plans.

Does this replace an avian vet?
No—this is educational planning support.

Why does storage guidance vary?
Because dry foods and fresh mixes behave differently. Paws & Plates shows storage tips based on recipe type.

Meta title: Bearded Dragon Feeding Schedule: What to Feed by Age | Paws & Plates
Meta description: A practical bearded dragon diet and schedule guide: staple foods, variety foods, what to avoid, and meal prep tips—without the confusion.

H1

Bearded dragon feeding schedule: what to feed and how to build a routine

Intro
Bearded dragons are one of the most searched reptile pets because feeding feels complicated: insects vs greens, schedule changes with age, and endless conflicting lists. This guide gives you a simple routine structure and safe planning principles.

Quick answer (TL;DR)

Build a routine around staple foods + controlled variety.

Feeding schedules change with life stage—don’t copy a plan meant for a different age.

Keep it consistent and observe body condition and appetite.

Use Paws & Plates to generate example meals and keep “staples vs treats” clear.

Safe staples (the “repeatable core”)

A strong plan separates:

Staples (the consistent base of the routine)

Variety (rotation items so you’re not repeating one thing forever)

Staples vary by age and individual setup; the key is building a repeatable pattern you can follow weekly.

Foods to limit

Very watery or low-value “filler” foods as the bulk of the routine

Novel foods introduced too frequently (makes it hard to see what works)

Anything you can’t identify reliably (unknown wild plants, etc.)

Foods to avoid (high-confidence)

Keep your avoid list conservative and based on clear hazards:

Anything spoiled or left out too long

Highly seasoned/processed human foods

Toxic or unknown plants

Simple schedule framework (no exact grams)

Instead of exact numbers (which depend on age/size/setup), use this structure:

Build a weekly plan with a consistent “staple rotation”

Keep variety foods as a smaller, planned part of the schedule

Introduce new foods one at a time

Meal prep + storage

Prep wash/chop items safely

Store fresh components appropriately

Don’t leave perishable items sitting out indefinitely

Switching foods

Transition gradually, and don’t change five things at once—you’ll never know what caused improvement/problems.
